Irrigation Methods

Surface Irrigation

How it works: Water is applied to the land and allowed to flow over the soil's surface by gravity.
Methods: Includes furrow, strip, and border irrigation, where water moves through trenches or channels between crop rows.
Pros: Cost-effective and suitable for large areas.
Cons: Less efficient and can lead to significant water loss.

Sprinkler Irrigation

How it works: Water is sprayed through overhead sprinklers, simulating natural rainfall.
Methods: Includes fixed or moving sprinkler systems, such as the large circular pattern of Center Pivot Irrigation.
Pros: Suitable for various soil types and climates, delivering water efficiently.
Cons: Can be energy-intensive and prone to wind drift.

Drip Irrigation

How it works: Water is delivered in small drops directly to the plant roots.
Pros: Extremely efficient, conserves water, and delivers nutrients directly to plants.
Cons: Requires higher initial investment and more maintenance.

Subsurface Irrigation

How it works: The water table is artificially raised from below to provide moisture to the root zone.
Pros: Pros: Prevents surface evaporation and is useful in some environments.
Cons: Only suitable where groundwater levels are controllable and high.
